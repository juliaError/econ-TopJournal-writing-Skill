# Introduction Figures

## Admission Rule

A figure belongs in the introduction only if it helps the reader understand the paper's question, setting, puzzle, or identifying variation before seeing the formal design.

## Good Introduction Figure Types

- A striking descriptive fact that motivates the question.
- A policy or institutional timeline that explains the design.
- A map or exposure distribution that makes the setting legible.
- A simple pre-period trend that motivates treatment/control comparison.
- A compact event-time preview if it clarifies the design and does not duplicate the main result section.

## What To Avoid

- A robustness figure in the introduction.
- A mechanism figure before the main result is known.
- A decorative map or trend with no identifying or motivational role.
- A figure whose ambiguity weakens the paper's core story.

## Design Rules

- Keep introduction figures simpler than result-section figures.
- Use at most two focal colors unless the figure is a map or distribution.
- Avoid dense legends; direct labels are better when space allows.
- Make the policy date, threshold, or group distinction visible.

## Check

If the introduction still works without the figure, the figure probably belongs later or in the appendix.
