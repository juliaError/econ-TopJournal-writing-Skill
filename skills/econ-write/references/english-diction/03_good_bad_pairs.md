# English Good-Bad Rewrite Pairs

## Principle

These are original examples for revision practice. They are not copied from the source papers. Use them to remove AI-like wording, translationese, vague aims, empty contributions, and overclaiming.

## Pair 1. Vague Aim

- Use when: abstract or introduction starts with a broad intention.
- Bad: `This paper aims to conduct an in-depth investigation of an important economic issue.`
- Better: `This paper studies whether [X] changes [Y] in [setting].`
- Check: replace "aims to" with the actual research action.

## Pair 2. Empty Contribution

- Use when: contribution sentence is generic.
- Bad: `This paper enriches the literature and has important theoretical and practical significance.`
- Better: `The paper adds evidence on [mechanism] by studying [setting/data/design].`
- Check: the contribution must name the margin added.

## Pair 3. Method As Main Point

- Use when: the sentence advertises a method without a question.
- Bad: `This paper uses a difference-in-differences model to analyze the problem.`
- Better: `I use the policy's staggered rollout to compare [treated units] with [control units].`
- Check: identify the variation, not just the estimator.

## Pair 4. Result Without Direction

- Use when: results are described only as significant.
- Bad: `The results show that the policy has a significant effect.`
- Better: `The policy reduces [Y] by [amount], relative to [baseline].`
- Check: report sign, magnitude, and benchmark.

## Pair 5. Overclaiming

- Use when: the prose says "prove" or "fully reveal."
- Bad: `The results fully prove the mechanism proposed in this paper.`
- Better: `The evidence is consistent with [mechanism], especially among [group where mechanism should be strongest].`
- Check: empirical evidence supports, estimates, tests, or is consistent with; it rarely proves.

## Pair 6. Translationese Topic Sentence

- Use when: a Chinese-style topic sentence is translated too literally.
- Bad: `With the rapid development of the economy, [topic] has attracted wide attention.`
- Better: `[Specific change] altered [actor]'s incentives to [action].`
- Check: start with the economic object, not a broad era phrase.

## Pair 7. Weak Literature Gap

- Use when: prior work is dismissed vaguely.
- Bad: `However, few studies have paid attention to this issue.`
- Better: `Existing work identifies [margin A], but says less about [margin B].`
- Check: name what the literature already did before naming the missing margin.

## Pair 8. Model Without Intuition

- Use when: theory prose jumps from equations to claims.
- Bad: `After solving the model, Proposition 1 is obtained.`
- Better: `The key force is that [friction] changes [choice], which gives rise to Proposition 1.`
- Check: state the economic force before or immediately after the formal result.

## Pair 9. Mechanical Robustness

- Use when: robustness is a list.
- Bad: `A series of robustness checks are conducted, and the conclusions remain unchanged.`
- Better: `The estimate remains similar when I change [sample/measure/specification], suggesting that the result is not driven by [concern].`
- Check: say which concern the check addresses.

## Pair 10. Formulaic Roadmap

- Use when: roadmap is generic.
- Bad: `The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces data. Section 3 reports results.`
- Better: `The next section describes the institutional change that generates the comparison used in the empirical design.`
- Check: a roadmap should preview the argument, not just list section titles.
