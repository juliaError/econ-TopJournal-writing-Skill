# English Economics Sentence Functions

## Principle

Edit by function first, wording second. A sentence in an economics paper should usually do one job: state the question, set up the environment, identify variation, report a result, interpret a mechanism, position the contribution, limit the claim, or guide the reader.

## 1. Research Question

- Use when: abstract opening, introduction question paragraph, section opening.
- Recommended: name the economic object and the relation being studied.
- Avoid: announcing that the paper "aims to investigate" a broad topic.
- Check: after one sentence, can the reader say what X, Y, and the setting are?
- Original example: `This paper asks whether [policy/shock] changes [outcome] for [population] in [setting].`

## 2. Theory Setup

- Use when: opening a model, defining players, timing, information, payoffs, or constraints.
- Recommended: introduce the economic environment in plain language before symbols.
- Avoid: starting with notation before the reader knows who chooses what.
- Check: can the reader describe the agents, choices, and friction without reading equations?
- Original example: `The model has [agents] who choose [action] after observing [information] and facing [constraint].`

## 3. Assumption And Equilibrium

- Use when: stating assumptions, equilibrium definitions, propositions, lemmas, or comparative statics.
- Recommended: explain what the formal object means economically.
- Avoid: treating assumptions as purely technical decoration.
- Check: does each assumption earn a role in the main mechanism?
- Original example: `Assumption 1 restricts [object] so that [mechanism] can be isolated from [confound].`

## 4. Identification And Variation

- Use when: empirical strategy, abstract method sentence, introduction design paragraph.
- Recommended: state the variation, comparison, and identifying object.
- Avoid: saying only that the paper "uses a regression" or "employs a model."
- Check: can the reader see where identifying variation comes from?
- Original example: `I identify the effect using variation in [treatment] across [units] and over [time].`

## 5. Data And Measurement

- Use when: defining data, variables, sample restrictions, constructed measures.
- Recommended: give unit, period, source, and measurement object early.
- Avoid: referring to "the data" before saying what they observe.
- Check: can a reader reconstruct the sample and key variable definitions?
- Original example: `The analysis uses [source], covering [units] from [years], with [outcome] measured as [definition].`

## 6. Result And Magnitude

- Use when: abstract findings, introduction result paragraphs, table interpretation.
- Recommended: report direction, size, uncertainty, and economic meaning in layers.
- Avoid: writing only that results are "significant" or "robust."
- Check: would the sentence still be useful if the word "significant" were deleted?
- Original example: `A [unit] increase in [X] is associated with a [size] change in [Y], relative to a mean of [baseline].`

## 7. Mechanism And Intuition

- Use when: explaining why a model result or empirical pattern arises.
- Recommended: connect incentives, constraints, behavior, and outcomes.
- Avoid: replacing mechanism with "this may be because of various channels."
- Check: is there a named actor and an action in each step?
- Original example: `[Shock] changes [actor]'s incentive to [action], which in turn affects [outcome].`

## 8. Literature And Contribution

- Use when: introduction contribution paragraph, related literature section.
- Recommended: state what prior work explains and what margin remains.
- Avoid: "This paper fills a gap" without saying which gap.
- Check: is the contribution about an object, mechanism, data source, identification design, or external validity?
- Original example: `Relative to work on [literature], this paper adds evidence on [margin] using [design/data].`

## 9. Limitation And Scope

- Use when: conclusion, robustness discussion, external validity paragraph.
- Recommended: bound the claim without weakening the core result.
- Avoid: universal claims such as "proves that" or "fully explains."
- Check: does the sentence state the relevant population, period, mechanism, or design limit?
- Original example: `The estimates speak most directly to [population/setting], where [condition] holds.`

## 10. Roadmap And Reader Guidance

- Use when: short roadmap, transition between main blocks.
- Recommended: point to specific content, not generic section labels.
- Avoid: a mechanical travelogue.
- Check: does the roadmap help the reader anticipate the argument?
- Original example: `The next section describes the policy change that generates the identifying variation.`
