# Theory And Empirical Prose

## Principle

Theory prose and empirical prose should sound different because they do different work. Theory prose clarifies an environment and intuition. Empirical prose clarifies variation, measurement, magnitude, and interpretation.

## Theory Prose

### 1. Setup Before Notation

- Use when: beginning a model section.
- Recommended: introduce agents, actions, timing, and information in prose before formal notation.
- Avoid: opening with a dense block of primitives.
- Check: can the setup paragraph be understood without equations?
- Original example: `The economy contains [agents] who choose [action] after observing [signal].`

### 2. Assumptions With Economic Content

- Use when: stating assumptions.
- Recommended: explain what the assumption rules in or out.
- Avoid: listing assumptions without economic interpretation.
- Check: does the text say whether the assumption is essential or simplifying?
- Original example: `The assumption isolates [force] by holding [confound] fixed.`

### 3. Proposition With Intuition

- Use when: presenting a theorem, proposition, lemma, or corollary.
- Recommended: state the result, then explain the force behind it.
- Avoid: "It is easy to show" as a substitute for intuition.
- Check: could a reader predict the comparative static after reading the intuition?
- Original example: `The proposition says that [outcome] rises with [parameter] because [incentive/constraint] becomes stronger.`

### 4. Comparative Statics As Economics

- Use when: explaining derivatives or parameter changes.
- Recommended: translate sign changes into behavior and trade-offs.
- Avoid: reporting only the sign of an expression.
- Check: does the sentence name the actor whose behavior changes?
- Original example: `When [parameter] increases, [actor] chooses less [action] because [cost/benefit] changes.`

## Empirical Prose

### 5. Identification Before Estimator

- Use when: empirical strategy section.
- Recommended: explain the comparison before naming the regression.
- Avoid: leading with model labels.
- Check: can the reader identify treatment, comparison group, and timing?
- Original example: `The design compares [treated units] with [controls] before and after [shock].`

### 6. Data As Measurement, Not Storage

- Use when: data section.
- Recommended: describe what the data observe and how variables map to concepts.
- Avoid: "The data are from..." without unit, period, or measurement.
- Check: can the reader map variables to economic concepts?
- Original example: `I measure [concept] using [variable], observed at the [unit] level from [years].`

### 7. Results In Three Layers

- Use when: interpreting tables.
- Recommended: write coefficient, magnitude, and interpretation separately if needed.
- Avoid: collapsing everything into "positive and significant."
- Check: result sentence includes sign; next sentence gives scale or meaning.
- Original example: `Column 2 shows a negative effect. The magnitude equals [benchmark], which suggests [interpretation].`

### 8. Robustness As Threat-Specific

- Use when: reporting robustness checks.
- Recommended: name the threat addressed by the check.
- Avoid: "all robustness checks pass."
- Check: each check corresponds to a concrete concern.
- Original example: `Dropping [units] addresses the concern that [specific group] drives the estimate.`

### 9. Mechanisms As Evidence, Not Certainty

- Use when: mechanism section.
- Recommended: connect subgroup, auxiliary outcome, or timing evidence to the mechanism.
- Avoid: claiming the mechanism is proven.
- Check: is the evidence necessary for the mechanism interpretation?
- Original example: `The response is concentrated among [group], where [mechanism] should be strongest.`
