# English Economics Verbs And Collocations

## Principle

Prefer plain verbs that reveal the paper's actual operation. Do not dress up weak claims with impressive verbs. A strong economics sentence usually has a concrete subject, a precise verb, and an observable object.

## Core Research Verbs

| Function | Recommended | Use Carefully | Avoid |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Research question | `study`, `ask`, `examine` | `investigate` | `aim to explore` |
| Estimation | `estimate`, `compare`, `test` | `evaluate` | `conduct an empirical analysis on` |
| Identification | `identify`, `exploit variation`, `use variation` | `leverage` | `prove causality through` |
| Descriptive facts | `document`, `show`, `describe` | `reveal` | `uncover profound patterns` |
| Theory | `derive`, `characterize`, `show`, `imply` | `rationalize` | `construct a sophisticated model to` |
| Mechanism | `operate through`, `work through`, `affect` | `drive` | `form a mechanism loop` |
| Contribution | `extend`, `complement`, `add evidence on` | `fill a gap` | `make an important contribution to` |

## Collocation Rules

### 1. Estimate Effects, Not Topics

- Use when: empirical result and abstract sentences.
- Recommended: `estimate the effect of [X] on [Y]`.
- Avoid: `estimate the relationship about [topic]`.
- Check: does the verb take an estimable object?
- Original example: `I estimate the effect of credit access on firm investment.`

### 2. Exploit Variation Only When Variation Is Real

- Use when: policy timing, thresholds, shocks, random assignment, instruments.
- Recommended: `exploit variation in [treatment] across [units/time]`.
- Avoid: `exploit the dataset` or `exploit the model`.
- Check: can you name the source of variation immediately after the verb?
- Original example: `The design exploits staggered eligibility across cohorts.`

### 3. Document Facts, Identify Effects

- Use when: separating descriptive and causal claims.
- Recommended: `document a pattern`; `identify an effect`.
- Avoid: using `identify` for descriptive correlations.
- Check: does the claim require a causal design?
- Original example: `The first result documents a sharp increase in borrowing; the second estimates its effect on consumption.`

### 4. Show Is Strong Enough

- Use when: theory or empirical results.
- Recommended: `show that`, `the results show`.
- Avoid: `demonstrate conclusively`, `prove empirically`.
- Check: would a referee object to the strength of the verb?
- Original example: `The model shows that coordination motives can overturn the private-value benchmark.`

### 5. Contribute By Adding A Margin

- Use when: contribution and literature paragraphs.
- Recommended: `adds evidence on [margin]`; `extends [literature] to [setting]`.
- Avoid: `enriches the literature` as a standalone claim.
- Check: can the contribution be stated without "important"?
- Original example: `The paper adds evidence on how firms adjust input choices after a credit shock.`

### 6. Interpret Results With Scale

- Use when: table discussion and abstract findings.
- Recommended: `corresponds to`, `is equivalent to`, `amounts to`, `relative to`.
- Avoid: `has a huge impact` or `has remarkable significance`.
- Check: does the sentence give a baseline or economically interpretable unit?
- Original example: `The estimate amounts to 12 percent of the pre-reform mean.`

### 7. Use May For Interpretation, Not Main Results

- Use when: discussing mechanisms or external validity.
- Recommended: `This pattern is consistent with [mechanism]`.
- Avoid: weakening a core estimate with unnecessary `may`.
- Check: is the sentence reporting an estimate or interpreting it?
- Original example: `The result is consistent with a liquidity channel, but it does not rule out demand-side adjustment.`
