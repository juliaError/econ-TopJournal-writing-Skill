# From Results To Outline

Use this file to turn a result package into a paper spine before writing prose.

## Result Ledger

For each table or figure, record:

- object: what relationship or fact it shows;
- role: descriptive fact, main result, mechanism, heterogeneity, robustness, placebo, or appendix support;
- outcome and main regressor;
- sample and specification;
- sign, magnitude, uncertainty, and economic interpretation;
- whether it belongs in the main text or appendix;
- caveat that must be stated near the result.

Do not treat every output as equally important. A full draft should be organized around the smallest set of evidence needed to answer the research question.

## Build The Spine

Convert the ledger into one chain:

1. Question: what economic relationship or mechanism is being explained?
2. Tension: why is the answer not obvious?
3. Design or model: what comparison, variation, or theoretical structure identifies the answer?
4. Main result: what is the central estimate or implication?
5. Mechanism: why does the result arise?
6. Boundary: where does the evidence apply, and where does it not?
7. Contribution: what changes in the reader's understanding?

## Table And Figure Placement

Use `econ-table-figure-design` to classify outputs:

- main text: one or two tables/figures that carry the central result;
- main text support: descriptive or mechanism outputs that make the story credible;
- appendix: alternative specifications, detailed construction checks, secondary heterogeneity, and long robustness grids;
- omit from draft: outputs that are visually confusing, contradict the design without explanation, or add no interpretive value.

## Outline Rule

Each section must answer one question:

- Introduction: why this question, why this design, what is found, and why it matters.
- Literature: what conversation this paper enters and what margin it changes.
- Setting or theory: what institutions or mechanism make the hypothesis plausible.
- Data and empirical strategy: what is measured and why the comparison is credible.
- Results: what the core evidence says.
- Mechanisms and heterogeneity: why and for whom the result appears.
- Robustness: whether the result survives credible alternative choices.
- Conclusion: what the evidence implies and what it does not settle.

If a table or paragraph does not answer one of these questions, move it to the appendix or mark it for deletion.
